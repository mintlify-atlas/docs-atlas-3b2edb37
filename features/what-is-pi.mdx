The **What is Pi?** section of the app gives you an interactive way to experience the fundamental relationship between a circle's circumference and its diameter. Rather than just stating that π ≈ 3.14159, the visualizer lets you drag a slider to resize a circle and watch the circumference recompute instantly — demonstrating that no matter the size, dividing circumference by diameter always yields the same irrational number.

## Interactive slider

The visualizer renders an animated circle alongside a range input. Dragging the slider changes the radius from a minimum of **10** to a maximum of **100** units. As you move it, two values update live in the readout above the slider:

- **Radius** — the current value you have selected
- **Diameter** — always `radius × 2`

The circle itself scales smoothly using a Framer Motion spring animation, so you can see the geometry grow and shrink in real time.

## Circumference formula

Below the slider, a display panel shows the computed circumference and the ratio that proves π:

```
Circumference = 2 × π × radius
```

For example, with a radius of 50 the panel reads:

```
Circumference = 314.16
314.16 / 100 = 3.14159...
```

The ratio `circumference / diameter` is always shown to five decimal places. Regardless of where you drag the slider, the result converges to π, making the constant's universality tangible rather than abstract.

<Note>
  The circumference is computed in JavaScript as `(2 * Math.PI * radius).toFixed(2)`, so you are seeing the browser's 64-bit floating-point approximation of π — already accurate to at least 15 significant digits.
</Note>

## Pi property cards

To the right of the visualizer, three cards explain what makes π special as a number. Each card contains an icon, a title, and a description pulled directly from the source:

<CardGroup cols={1}>
  <Card title="Irrational (Es Irracional)" icon="infinity">
    Pi cannot be expressed as an exact fraction (such as a/b). Its decimal digits continue forever without repeating in any pattern.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Transcendental (Es Trascendente)" icon="sparkles">
    Pi is not the root of any algebraic equation with rational coefficients. This means it is impossible to "square the circle" using only a straightedge and compass.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Universal (Es Universal)" icon="globe">
    Pi appears in formulas from quantum physics, electromagnetism, cosmology, and even in the probability that a needle crosses a line — the Buffon's needle problem.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

Each card lifts and slides slightly to the right when you hover over it, giving the page a responsive, tactile feel.

## Why the ratio is always π

The core insight the section conveys is this: no matter how large or small the circle is, the ratio of its circumference to its diameter is always π. The slider makes this experiential — you can set the radius to 10, note the ratio, set it to 100, and see the same value appear. This constancy is precisely what makes π a mathematical constant rather than a measurement.

<Info>
  The value 355/113 — introduced later in the History timeline — is so close to π (accurate to six decimal places) that it was the best known approximation for 800 years. Yet even that fraction is only an approximation; π's irrationality guarantees no fraction can be exact.
</Info>
