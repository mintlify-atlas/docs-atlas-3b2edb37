Pi isn't just an abstract mathematical curiosity — it underpins real-world science, engineering, and nature in ways you encounter every day. Wherever there are circles, curves, waves, or orbits, Pi is at work. The app highlights six domains where Pi is indispensable.

## Applications

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="Space science" icon="rocket">
    NASA uses only 15 decimal places of Pi to navigate rovers on Mars and send probes to the edge of the solar system with millimeter precision.
  </Card>
  <Card title="GPS and satellites" icon="satellite">
    Orbit calculations and global positioning depend entirely on the spherical geometry of the Earth, where Pi is the central constant.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Waves and signals" icon="wave-sine">
    The Fourier transform — used to compress MP3s, process images, and analyze Wi-Fi signals — contains Pi in its fundamental equation.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Architecture" icon="building">
    From designing massive domes to estimating materials for cylinders and arches, Pi ensures structural integrity across countless engineering projects.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Quantum physics" icon="atom">
    Heisenberg's uncertainty principle and the Schrödinger equation both use Pi to describe the behavior of particles at the quantum level.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Rivers and nature" icon="map">
    The ratio between a river's actual winding length and the straight-line distance from its source to its mouth tends, surprisingly, toward Pi.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>
